# Audit vs. Review vs. Compilation: Key Differences Explained

When business owners prepare financial statements, they often hear three terms: **audit, review, and compilation**. While they sound similar, each service provides a very different level of assurance.

In this guide, we’ll explain the differences, when each is needed, and how to decide which one is right for your company.

---

![audit-vs-review-vs-compilation.webp](/images/blog/audit-vs-review-vs-compilation.webp)

## Why the Confusion?

The terms get mixed up because all three services involve financial statements prepared by an accountant or auditor. The difference lies in:

- The **level of assurance** (confidence in the numbers).
- The **procedures performed**.
- The **cost and time required**.

---

## Audit: The Highest Level of Assurance

An **audit** is the most thorough service. An independent auditor examines your company’s financial statements and supporting evidence to provide an opinion on whether the numbers are accurate and comply with accounting standards.

**Key features:**

- ✔️ Required by law in many countries (e.g., Malta, Cyprus, most EU states above thresholds).
- ✔️ Auditor tests transactions, confirms balances, and reviews internal controls.
- ✔️ Provides “reasonable assurance” that the accounts are free from material misstatement.

**When it’s needed:**

- When required by legislation (statutory audits).
- When banks, investors, or regulators demand full confidence.
- For companies seeking credibility with external stakeholders.

---

## Review: Limited Assurance

A **review** is less extensive than an audit. The accountant or auditor performs limited procedures (such as inquiries and analytical reviews) to check if anything appears incorrect.

**Key features:**

- ⚖️ Provides “limited assurance” (lower than an audit).
- ⚖️ No testing of transactions or verification of balances.
- ⚖️ Costs less and takes less time than a full audit.

**When it’s used:**

- For smaller businesses not legally required to undergo an audit.
- When stakeholders only need moderate comfort, not full assurance.

---

## Compilation: No Assurance

A **compilation** is the simplest service. The accountant helps prepare financial statements based on the company’s data — but does not provide any assurance.

**Key features:**

- 📝 No opinion, no testing, no assurance.
- 📝 Simply presents financial information in a structured format.
- 📝 Lowest cost option.

**When it’s used:**

- For internal use only (management purposes).
- For small businesses just starting out.
- When owners need statements for tax filings but no third-party reliance.

---

## Side-by-Side Comparison

| **Service** | **Assurance Provided** | **Procedures** | **Typical Cost** | **When Needed** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Audit** | High (“reasonable assurance”) | Tests, confirmations, full review | Highest | Legal requirement, banks, investors |
| **Review** | Moderate (“limited assurance”) | Inquiries, analytics | Medium | SMEs not required to audit |
| **Compilation** | None | Prepares statements only | Lowest | Internal use, tax filings |

---

## International Perspective

- **Malta & Cyprus**: Audits are required for all companies, no review/compilation exemptions.
- **UK & Ireland**: SMEs may qualify for audit exemption; reviews/compilations are common alternatives.
- **USA**: All three services are widely used, with clear distinctions in reporting standards.

👉 Key takeaway: Your location often determines which service is available to you.
