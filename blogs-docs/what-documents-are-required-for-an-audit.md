For many businesses, the word "audit" immediately creates stress, usually because companies are unsure what information auditors will ask for and how much preparation is actually involved.

In reality, most audits follow a fairly structured process. Auditors are primarily trying to verify that the financial statements accurately reflect the company's financial position and that the supporting records behind those numbers are complete, reliable, and properly documented.

The smoother the documentation process is, the smoother the audit usually becomes.

One of the biggest misconceptions businesses have is that audit preparation starts once auditors request documents. In practice, audit readiness is something that develops throughout the year through organized bookkeeping, reconciliations, recordkeeping, and operational discipline.

A large portion of audit delays are not caused by technical accounting problems. They are caused by missing documents, incomplete explanations, scattered files, or difficulty retrieving information quickly.

Understanding what auditors typically request can help businesses prepare earlier and reduce unnecessary pressure during the audit process.

## Why Auditors Request Documentation

Auditors do not simply review financial statements at face value. They need supporting evidence behind the figures presented in the accounts.

For example, if a company reports €500,000 in revenue, auditors need documentation showing that the revenue genuinely exists and was recorded correctly. If the company reports cash balances, liabilities, expenses, inventory, or loans, auditors will normally request supporting records to verify them.

This process is part of obtaining what is known as "audit evidence."

The quality and organization of documentation can significantly affect how efficiently an audit progresses.

## Core Financial Documents Usually Requested

Although every audit differs depending on the business and industry, there are several documents auditors commonly request in most engagements.

### Trial Balance

The trial balance is one of the main starting points of an audit. It contains the balances of all ledger accounts at the financial year-end and forms the basis of the financial statements.

Auditors use the trial balance to identify material balances, unusual movements, and areas requiring further testing.

### General Ledger

The general ledger provides detailed transaction-level activity behind each account balance.

For example, if auditors are reviewing marketing expenses or director loans, they will often inspect the underlying ledger entries to understand how balances were built throughout the year.

### Bank Statements and Bank Reconciliations

Bank documentation is one of the most important areas during an audit.

Auditors typically request:

- Bank statements covering the full financial year
- Year-end bank statements
- Bank reconciliations
- Supporting explanations for unusual transactions
- Details of loans or financing arrangements

Bank reconciliations help auditors verify that accounting records match actual bank activity.

Missing or outdated reconciliations are one of the most common issues businesses face during audits.

## Sales and Revenue Documentation

Revenue is usually considered a high-risk audit area because it directly affects profitability.

Auditors may request:

- Sales invoices
- Customer agreements
- Contracts
- Revenue reports
- Credit notes
- Supporting schedules for major clients

If there are significant changes in revenue compared to previous years, auditors may also ask management to explain the reasons behind those fluctuations.

For example, businesses experiencing rapid growth, major client losses, or unusual one-time projects may need to provide additional supporting explanations.

## Purchase and Expense Documentation

To verify expenses and liabilities, auditors commonly request:

- Supplier invoices
- Expense receipts
- Payment confirmations
- Creditors listings
- Accrual schedules
- Supporting agreements

Auditors may perform sample testing on expenses to confirm they are business-related, properly recorded, and supported by documentation.

Large or unusual expenses often receive additional attention.

## VAT and Tax Documentation

For companies registered for VAT, auditors will usually request:

- VAT returns
- VAT working schedules
- Tax computations
- Corporate tax filings
- Supporting reconciliations

This helps ensure that tax balances in the financial statements agree with submitted filings and accounting records.

Inconsistent VAT records are one of the areas that can create avoidable audit complications if not maintained properly throughout the year.

## Payroll Records

For businesses with employees, payroll is another major audit area.

Auditors may request:

- Payroll summaries
- Payslips
- Employment contracts
- Social security records
- Tax deductions
- Payroll reconciliations

If payroll balances increase significantly during the year or if the company has complex compensation structures, additional supporting documentation may also be requested.

## Fixed Assets and Depreciation Schedules

If the company owns equipment, vehicles, machinery, furniture, or other assets, auditors typically review:

- Fixed asset registers
- Asset purchase invoices
- Depreciation schedules
- Disposal documentation

Auditors may also verify whether depreciation calculations are reasonable and consistently applied.

## Debtors and Creditors Listings

Trade receivables and payables are commonly reviewed during audits.

Businesses may be asked to provide:

- Aged debtors listings
- Aged creditors listings
- Customer balances
- Supplier balances
- Supporting reconciliations

In some cases, auditors may also send confirmation requests directly to customers or suppliers to verify balances independently.

## Inventory Documentation

For companies holding inventory, stock records become especially important.

Auditors may request:

- Inventory listings
- Stock count reports
- Valuation methodologies
- Inventory movement reports
- Supporting costing calculations

Inventory is often one of the more sensitive audit areas because inaccuracies can significantly affect profitability and financial reporting.

## Corporate and Legal Documents

Auditors also commonly request corporate documentation such as:

- Company incorporation documents
- Shareholder records
- Board meeting minutes
- Loan agreements
- Lease agreements
- Significant contracts
- Financing arrangements

These documents help auditors understand the legal and operational structure of the business.

## Common Problems Businesses Face During Audits

Many businesses actually have the required information but struggle because records are poorly organized.

Common issues include:

- Missing invoices
- Incomplete reconciliations
- Documents spread across multiple systems
- Reliance on email searches
- Unclear approval processes
- Last-minute preparation
- Missing explanations for unusual transactions

This often creates delays, repeated requests, and operational pressure during the audit.

In practice, companies with structured financial processes usually experience far smoother audits than businesses relying heavily on manual or fragmented workflows.

## Why Audit Preparation Should Be Ongoing

One mistake businesses frequently make is treating audit preparation as a year-end task.

The most efficient audits usually happen when:

- reconciliations are updated regularly,
- documents are organized consistently,
- records are maintained throughout the year,
- and financial information is reviewed periodically rather than only before deadlines.

Modern accounting systems and centralized document workflows have made this process significantly easier compared to traditional manual approaches.

Businesses that maintain operational discipline throughout the year often reduce both audit delays and internal stress significantly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How early should businesses prepare for an audit?

Ideally, preparation should begin throughout the financial year rather than only after year-end. However, most businesses should start reviewing records several weeks before the audit begins.

### Do small businesses need the same documents as large companies?

The core principles are similar, but larger businesses usually require more extensive supporting documentation due to operational complexity.

### What happens if documents are missing?

Missing records can delay the audit and may result in additional audit procedures being required. In some cases, insufficient documentation can also affect audit conclusions.

### Are digital documents acceptable?

Yes. Most modern audits accept digital documentation, provided records are organized, accessible, and reliable.

## Conclusion

Audits are not simply about financial statements themselves. They are about the evidence supporting those statements.

Businesses that maintain organized records, consistent reconciliations, and structured documentation processes throughout the year generally experience far smoother audits and fewer delays.

While every company's requirements differ depending on size and complexity, strong documentation remains one of the most important foundations of an efficient audit process.
